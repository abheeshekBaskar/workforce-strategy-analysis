# Case Study 1: Performance Appraisal Politics

> *Based on "Behind the Mask: The Politics of Employee Appraisal"*

---

## Overview

This analysis examines how organizational politics influence performance appraisals — and why the traditional academic goal of achieving purely "accurate" appraisals may be fundamentally unrealistic.

---

## Core Findings

### 1. What Is the Basic Theme?

The central argument of the article is that **organizational politics and managerial discretion — not accuracy — are the primary drivers of executive behavior during performance appraisals.**

Through in-depth interviews with executives, the authors found that most deliberately distort ratings (through inflation or deflation) to achieve political goals rather than reflecting true performance. These goals include:

- Maintaining interpersonal relationships with subordinates
- Avoiding unnecessary conflict
- Motivating and rewarding employees through discretion
- Protecting employees experiencing personal hardship
- Preventing internal departmental issues from becoming public

The authors argue that since politics are an inevitable organizational reality, the academic focus on "eliminating" political bias from appraisals misses the point. Instead, they advocate for **judiciously managing** political dynamics through systemic design choices.

---

### 2. Politics as a Reality of Organizational Life

The article openly acknowledges — through executive admissions — that politics are inescapable in performance evaluation. Three structural reasons why:

| Factor | Why It Drives Politics |
|--------|------------------------|
| Ongoing relationships | Managers must work with subordinates *after* the appraisal |
| Permanent documentation | Written appraisals create lasting records in personnel files |
| Consequential outcomes | Ratings tie directly to promotions, raises, and career trajectory |

**Analysis:** These aren't irrational behaviors. Executives are responding to real incentives and structural pressures. Calling it "bias" and trying to train it away misses the deeper systemic issue.

That said, political discretion must be applied *ethically*. Unchecked, it can produce appraisal systems where ratings reflect manager relationships more than actual performance — eroding fairness and trust.

**Recommended checks and balances:**
- Transparency in how ratings connect to outcomes
- Accountability mechanisms (e.g., calibration sessions)
- Manager training on ethical use of discretion
- Separation of developmental feedback from administrative ratings

---

### 3. Inflating Performance Appraisals

The article documents numerous motivations behind rating inflation:

- Maximizing raise eligibility for valued employees
- Protecting employees going through temporary personal crises
- Avoiding confrontation with low performers
- Rewarding recent improvement even if overall performance is mediocre

Importantly, **inflation predominantly occurred in overall ratings** rather than individual performance dimensions — suggesting executives use the summary score as a discretionary lever while maintaining more honest sub-ratings.

**Analysis:** Selective inflation has a logic to it. Blanket honesty in ratings can damage relationships, create legal exposure, and demotivate employees who are trending upward. But excessive inflation has costs too:

- Ratings lose informational value
- High performers feel undervalued when everyone gets inflated scores
- Feedback loops that could drive real improvement are cut short

---

### 4. Major Conclusion Finding

The study's most significant finding: **accuracy in appraisals was not the primary goal for the executives interviewed.** Their actual priorities were motivating employees and distributing rewards appropriately — with accuracy as a secondary concern at best.

This fundamentally challenges the assumptions underlying most HR appraisal system design, which is engineered for measurement precision (standardized scales, calibrated anchors, forced distributions) rather than managerial effectiveness.

**My position:** Eliminating politics from performance appraisals is neither possible nor fully desirable. The goal should be a system that:
1. Acknowledges political realities rather than pretending to eliminate them
2. Introduces accountability so political discretion doesn't become political abuse
3. Separates administrative ratings (used for compensation) from developmental feedback (used for growth) — reducing the political stakes of each

---

## Business Analysis Takeaway

For any organization redesigning its performance management system, the key insight is this: **design for how humans actually behave, not how textbooks say they should behave.**

Systems that ignore organizational politics don't eliminate them — they just push them underground, where they become less accountable and more harmful.
